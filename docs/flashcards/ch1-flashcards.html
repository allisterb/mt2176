MT2176 Further Calculus Flashcards: Limits of Functions

1 What is the intuitive definition of the limit of a function \(f(x)\) as \(x\) approaches \(a\), written as \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\)?

Answer:

It means that we can make the value of \(f(x)\) arbitrarily close to \(L\) by taking \(x\) to be sufficiently close to \(a\) (on either side of \(a\)) but not equal to \(a\).

Explanation:

The concept of a limit describes the behavior of a function near a point, not necessarily at the point itself. The function doesn't even need to be defined at \(x=a\) for the limit to exist. This is the foundational concept for defining continuity and derivatives.

Source: MT2176 Subject Guide, Section 2.1.2; Wrede, Chapter 3.

2 State the formal \(\epsilon-\delta\) definition of the limit: \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\).

Answer:

For every number \(\epsilon > 0\), there is a number \(\delta > 0\) such that if \(0 < |x - a| < \delta\), then \(|f(x) - L| < \epsilon\).

Explanation:

This definition makes the intuitive idea of 'arbitrarily close' precise. \(\epsilon\) represents the desired closeness to the limit \(L\), and the definition guarantees we can find a range (\(\delta\)) around \(a\) such that for any \(x\) in that range (except \(a\) itself), \(f(x)\) is within \(\epsilon\) of \(L\).

Source: Ostaszewski, Section 17.4; Wrede, Chapter 3.

3 What does it mean for a function \(f(x)\) to be continuous at a point \(x=c\)?

Answer:

A function \(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x=c\) if \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c)\).

Explanation:

This single statement implies three conditions: 1. The limit \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x)\) must exist. 2. The function must be defined at \(c\), i.e., \(f(c)\) exists. 3. The limit must be equal to the function's value. Graphically, this means there are no breaks, jumps, or holes at that point.

Source: MT2176 Subject Guide, Section 2.2.1; Wrede, Chapter 3.

4 What is the definition of the derivative of a function \(f(x)\) at a point \(x=a\)?

Answer:

The derivative of \(f\) at \(a\), denoted \(f'(a)\), is the limit: 
f'(a) = 
    lim_{h \to 0} rac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}
provided this limit exists.

Explanation:

The derivative represents the instantaneous rate of change of the function at a point. Geometrically, it is the slope of the tangent line to the graph of \(y=f(x)\) at the point \((a, f(a))\).

Source: Wrede, Chapter 4; Ostaszewski, Section 17.1.

5 What is the relationship between differentiability and continuity?

Answer:

If a function is differentiable at a point, then it must be continuous at that point.

Explanation:

Differentiability is a stronger condition than continuity. However, the converse is not true. A function can be continuous at a point but not differentiable there. A classic example is \(f(x) = |x|\) at \(x=0\), which is continuous but has a "sharp corner," so its derivative is undefined.

Source: MT2176 Subject Guide, Section 2.2.2; Wrede, Chapter 4.

6 When can L'Hôpital's Rule be applied to find \(\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\)?

Answer:

L'Hôpital's Rule can be applied if the limit results in an indeterminate form of type \(\frac{0}{0}\) or \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\), and \(\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}\) exists.

Explanation:

The rule states that if these conditions are met, then \(\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}\). It is a powerful tool for evaluating limits that are otherwise difficult to compute.

Source: MT2176 Subject Guide, Section 2.2.4; Ostaszewski, Section 18.8.

7 What is a Taylor series for a function \(f(x)\) about \(x=c\)?

Answer:

The Taylor series for \(f(x)\) about \(x=c\) is the power series: 

                sum_{n=0}^{\infty} rac{f^{(n)}(c)}{n!}(x-c)^n = f(c) + f'(c)(x-c) + rac{f''(c)}{2!}(x-c)^2 + ...

Explanation:

A Taylor series is a representation of a function as an infinite sum of terms, calculated from the values of the function's derivatives at a single point. It's used to approximate functions.

Source: MT2176 Subject Guide, Section 2.2.3; Wrede, Chapter 11.

8 Find the limit: \(\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}\).

Answer:

The limit is 4.

Explanation:

This is an indeterminate form \(\frac{0}{0}\). We can solve it by factoring the numerator: 

                lim_{x \to 2} rac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = lim_{x \to 2} (x+2) = 2+2 = 4
Alternatively, using L'Hôpital's Rule: 

                lim_{x \to 2} rac{2x}{1} = 2(2) = 4

Source: Wrede, Chapter 3, Solved Problems.

9 Use the definition of the derivative to find the derivative of \(f(x) = x^2\).

Answer:

\(f'(x) = 2x\).

Explanation:

Using the definition \(f'(x) = lim_{h \to 0} rac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}\): 

                lim_{h \to 0} rac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h} = lim_{h \to 0} rac{x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2}{h}
                

                = lim_{h \to 0} rac{2xh + h^2}{h} = lim_{h \to 0} (2x + h) = 2x

Source: Wrede, Chapter 4; Ostaszewski, Section 17.5.

10 What is Taylor's Theorem with the Lagrange form of the remainder?

Answer:

If \(f(x)\) has \(n+1\) derivatives on an interval containing \(c\) and \(x\), then 

                f(x) = P_n(x) + R_n(x)
where \(P_n(x)\) is the nth-degree Taylor polynomial and the remainder \(R_n(x)\) is given by 

                R_n(x) = rac{f^{(n+1)}(d)}{(n+1)!}(x-c)^{n+1}
for some number \(d\) between \(c\) and \(x\).

Explanation:

This theorem provides a way to quantify the error when approximating a function with its Taylor polynomial. The remainder term \(R_n(x)\) is the difference between the function and its nth-degree Taylor approximation.

Source: MT2176 Subject Guide, Section 2.2.3; Wrede, Chapter 11.
